As time goes by: is *now* a good time to talk about gun control?

Wed 18 Oct 2017, 1:07 PM AEDT

Every time there is a major mass shooting in the United States, there is a flurry of debate about gun control.

In the days following the latest - in which 59 people were shot dead by one man in Las Vegas - the US president Donald Trump said the country could debate tighter gun restrictions "as time goes by".

Likewise, other commentators told the public that the immediate aftermath of a shooting was "not the time" to be discussing gun laws.

But if history is a guide, there is a narrow window for acting on gun violence in the wake of an attack.

The "issue-attention cycle" was a phrase coined in the 1970s to describe the way in which the media - and by default, interest from the public - peaks for a short time following an event, and then fades. The story becomes less talked-about, we move on, and the chance to make some change disappears until the next time.

In previous eras, "media mentions" (the number of times a phrase was used in news articles or reports) were used to demonstrate this effect. But today we have Google Trends data, giving us an insight into what people are thinking about, minus the media filter.

When applied to terrorist attacks or shootings that gain global attention, it tells the same story again and again:

[worldwide joy plot]

And when we focus on US searches following mass shootings, we see a similar pattern. Here we have search data showing the searches around major US shootings. The only shooting to prompt a relatively large amount of "gun control" searches was the massacre of 26 people, including 20 children, at Sandy Hook Elementary School in Newtown, Connecticut.

[stacked pulsar chart]

[chart]

As several commentators have pointed out, if the massacre of primary school students at Sandy Hook Elementary School wasn't enough to force federal change on guns, what will?

The inability of the US Congress to deal with America's gun violence problem has prompted reform groups like Everytown to focus on smaller battles, with some quiet success.

They have their work cut out for them in the state of Nevada, the scene of the worst mass shooting in modern US history at the beginning of the month. Nevada has some of the country's least restrictive gun laws, and the right to bear arms is included in the state constitution.

Search data suggests public interest in the shooting has already waned:

[chart of several Las Vegas related search terms]

Using public interest as a guide, if leaders won't talk about gun violence in the aftermath of a mass shooting, they probably never will.
