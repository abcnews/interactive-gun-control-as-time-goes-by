As time goes by, we lose interest in the latest massacre

Mon 16 Oct 2017, 2:24 PM AEDT

Every time there is a major mass shooting in the United States, there is a flurry of debate about gun control.

In the days following the latest - in which 59 people were shot dead by one man in Las Vegas - the US president Donald Trump said the country could debate tighter gun restrictions "as time goes by".

Likewise, other commentators told the public that the immediate aftermath of a shooting was "not the time" to be discussing gun laws.

But if history is a guide, there is a narrow window for acting on gun violence in the wake of an attack.

The "issue-attention cycle" was a phrase coined in the 1970s to describe the way in which the media - and by default, interest from the public - peaks for a short time following an event, and then fades. The story becomes less talked-about, we move on, and the chance to make some change disappears until the next time.

In previous eras, "media mentions" (the number of times a phrase was used in news articles or reports) were used to demonstrate this effect. But today we have Google Trends data, giving us an insight into public interest, without the media filter.

When applied to terrorist attacks or shootings that gain global attention, it tells the same story again and again:

And when we focus on US searches following mass shootings, we see a similar pattern. Here we have search data showing two weeks either side of major US shootings.

Compared to the search for the name of the city, the search for "gun control" becomes smaller and smaller over time.

[chart]

And after the recent Las Vegas shootings, an interesting search popped up: Bump stocks are gun attachments that make a semi-automatic weapon mimic the firing of an automatic weapon. The Las Vegas shooter used bump stocks in the massacre at the Route 91 music festival and in the days following the attack, lawmakers started talking about a ban. Here's what happened on Google:

[chart: gun control, bump stocks]

This phenomenon is partly responsible for US gun control advocates shifting their focus from federal to state laws, as state reform is less reliant on huge media attention.

As several commentators have pointed out, if the massacre of primary school students at Sandy Hook Elementary School wasn't enough to force federal change on guns, what will?

And gun control campaigners have their work cut out for them in the state of Nevada, the scene of the worst mass shooting in modern US history at the beginning of the month. Nevada has some of the country's least restrictive gun laws, and the right to bear arms is included in the state constitution.
